//Initially the stone is immersed in air and could be considered a composite of stone and air.
//The air SO is a volume of air at the front of the volume of the SO water wave. The air is a mirror image of the wave.
//The Reynolds number of approx 5500 reflects the dominance of dynamic forces over viscosity implying that the reduction in velocity of the stone is 
//insignificant in the period before collision with the front of the wave.

//The wave SO is a volume of water from the front of the wave back into the wave (20cm * 20cm * 20cm). 
//The stone contacts the front of the wave and is at that moment a composite object of air, stone and water.
//The stone proceeds into the wave and at a certain moment is no longer in the air; it is fully immersed in the water.
//It is at that moment a composite with the wave.
//The Reynolds number of approx 100 reflects the relative importance of viscosity implying that the reduction in velocity of the stone is 
//significant in the couple of seconds after collision with the front of the wave.
//This scenario is similar to dropping a stone into a pool of water. Gravity plays a more important role and
//wave forming on the pool of water is easier to envisage than the waves which must also be present on the face of the wave as the stone enters it.


//Before the stone hits the wave is it a continuum and in the sense that it can be endlessly divided while remaining a wave.
//This is no longer the case when the stone collides with the wave.

//For the purposes of the script a closed system is created by choosing SOs which enclose any effects of the stone wave interaction.
